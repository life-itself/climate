Why are we discussing energy policy?

Three different motivations drive today‚Äôs energy discussions.

First, fossil fuels are a Ô¨Ånite resource. It seems possible that cheap oil (on which our cars and lorries run) and cheap gas (with which we heat many of our buildings) will run out in our lifetime. So we seek alternative energy sources. Indeed given that fossil fuels are a valuable resource, useful for manufacture of plastics and all sorts of other creative stuff, perhaps we should save them for better uses than simply setting Ô¨Åre to them.

Second, we‚Äôre interested in security of energy supply. (I hope you can hear my tongue in my cheek.) Going by Ô¨Ågure 1.2, it certainly looks as if ‚Äúour‚Äù fossil fuels have peaked. The UK has a particular security-of-supply problem looming, known as the ‚Äúenergy gap.‚Äù A substantial number of old coal power stations and nuclear power stations will be closing down during the next decade (Ô¨Ågure 1.3), so there is a risk that electricity demand will sometimes exceed electricity supply, if adequate plans are not implemented.

Third, it‚Äôs very probable that using fossil fuels changes the climate. Climate change is blamed on several human activities, but the biggest contributor to climate change is the increase in greenhouse effect produced by carbon dioxide (CO2). Most of the carbon dioxide emissions come from fossil-fuel burning. And the main reason we burn fossil fuels is for energy. So to Ô¨Åx climate change, we need to sort out a new way of getting energy. The climate problem is mostly an energy problem.

Whichever of these three concerns motivates you, we need energy numbers, and policies that add up.

The Ô¨Årst two concerns are straightforward selÔ¨Åsh motivations for drastically reducing fossil fuel use. The third concern, climate change, is a more altruistic motivation ‚Äì the brunt of climate change will be borne not by us but by future generations over many hundreds of years. OK. Fossil fuel burning increases CO2 concentrations signiÔ¨Åcantly. But does it matter? ‚ÄúCarbon is nature!‚Äù, the oilspinners remind us, ‚ÄúCarbon is life!‚Äù If CO2 had no harmful effects, then indeed carbon emissions would not matter. However, carbon dioxide is a greenhouse gas. Not the strongest greenhouse gas, but a signiÔ¨Åcant one nonetheless. Put more of it in the atmosphere, and it does what greenhouse gases do: it absorbs infrared radiation (heat) heading out from the earth and reemits it in a random direction; the effect of this random redirection of the atmospheric heat trafÔ¨Åc is to impede the Ô¨Çow of heat from the planet, just like a quilt. So carbon dioxide has a warming effect. 14 This fact is based not on complex historical records of global temperatures but on the simple physical properties of CO2 molecules. Greenhouse gases are a quilt, and CO2 is one layer of the quilt.

So, if humanity succeeds in doubling or tripling CO2 concentrations (which is where we are certainly heading, under business as usual), what happens? Here, there is a lot of uncertainty. Climate science is difÔ¨Åcult. The climate is a complex, twitchy beast, and exactly how much warming CO2 -doubling would produce is uncertain. The consensus of the best climate models seems to be that doubling the CO2 concentration would have roughly the same effect as increasing the intensity of the sun by 2%, and would bump up the global mean temperature by something like 3¬∞C. I won‚Äôt recite the whole litany of probable drastic effects, as I am sure you‚Äôve heard it before. 15 The litany begins ‚Äúthe Greenland icecap would gradually melt, and, over a period of a few 100 years, sea-level would rise by about 7 metres.‚Äù The brunt of the litany falls on future generations. Climate modelling is difÔ¨Åcult and is dogged by uncertainties. But uncertainty about exactly how the climate will respond to extra greenhouse gases is no justiÔ¨Åcation for inaction. OK, enough about climate change. I‚Äôm going to assume we are motivated to get off fossil fuels. Whatever your motivation, the aim of this book is to help you Ô¨Ågure out the numbers and do the arithmetic so that you can evaluate policies; and to lay a factual foundation so that you can see which proposals add up. I‚Äôm not claiming that the arithmetic and numbers in this book are new; the books I‚Äôve mentioned by Goodstein, Lomborg, and Lovelock, for example, are full of interesting numbers and back-of-envelope calculations, and there are many other helpful sources on the internet too. 20

What I‚Äôm aiming to do in this book is to make these numbers simple and memorable; to show you how you can Ô¨Ågure out the numbers for yourself; and to make the situation so clear that any thinking reader will be able to draw striking conclusions. I‚Äôd like to emphasize that the calculations we will do are deliberately imprecise. SimpliÔ¨Åcation is a key to understanding. First, by rounding the numbers, we can make them easier to remember. Second, rounded numbers allow quick calculations. For example, in this book, the population of the United Kingdom is 60 million, and the population of the world is 6 billion. For instance, if you learn that a roundtrip intercontinental Ô¨Çight emits nearly two tons of CO2 per passenger, then knowing the average emissions yardstick (5-and-a-bit tons per year per person) helps you realize that just one such plane-trip per year corresponds to over a third of the average person‚Äôs carbon emissions.

I like to base my calculations on everyday knowledge rather than on trawling through impersonal national statistics. For example, if I want to estimate the typical wind speeds in Cambridge, I ask ‚Äúis my cycling speed usually faster than the wind?‚Äù The answer is yes. I back up these everyday estimates with other peoples‚Äô calculations and with ofÔ¨Åcial statistics. (Please look for these in each chapter‚Äôs end-notes.) This book isn‚Äôt intended to be a deÔ¨Ånitive store of super-accurate numbers. Rather, it‚Äôs intended to illustrate how to use approximate numbers as a part of constructive consensual conversations.

In the calculations, I‚Äôll mainly use the United Kingdom and occasionally Europe, America, or the whole world, but you should Ô¨Ånd it easy to redo the calculations for whatever country or region you are interested in.

Let me close this chapter with a few more warnings to the reader. Not only will we make a habit of approximating the numbers we calculate; we‚Äôll also neglect all sorts of details that investors, managers, and economists have to attend to, poor folks. If you‚Äôre trying to launch a renewable technology, just a 5% increase in costs may make all the difference between success and failure, so in business every detail must be tracked. But 5% is too small for this book‚Äôs radar. This is a book about factors of 2 and factors of 10. It‚Äôs about physical limits to sustainable energy, not current economic feasibility. While economics is always changing, the fundamental limits won‚Äôt ever go away. We need to understand these limits.

But we don‚Äôt know whether they are all true. Some of them are currently judged ‚Äúvery likely.‚Äù The difÔ¨Åculty of deciding which factual assertions are true leads to debates in the scientiÔ¨Åc community. But given sufÔ¨Åcient scientiÔ¨Åc experiment and discussion, the truth or falsity of most factual assertions can eventually be resolved, at least ‚Äúbeyond reasonable doubt.‚Äù
